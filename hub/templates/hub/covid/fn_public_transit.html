Covid and our bus trips

Opal trips taken by adult card holders per month in the Sydney metropolitan area

What can we learn from the data at hand?

Since metropolitan adult Opal bus trips hit rock bottom in April of 2020, they had made a strong recovery (with a temporary dip due to the Nothern Beaches outbreak), rising an average of 608,589 trips a month until April of 2021. The Delta strain hit hard, canceling out any gains that had been made, bringing monthly Adult bus trips to their lowest point in August.

August 2021 recorded 1,838,385 adult card Opal trips in the Sydney metropolitan area. The average number of trip's from January of 2018 through to Feburary of 2020 were 14,446,509. Thus August's trips constituted approximately 13% of pre-Covid average trips.

Lastly, assuming we have the same average recovery speed of 608,589 trips a month, it would take just over 20 months for Opal bus trips to hit the pre-Covid average, meaning the Sydney's metropolitan bus system won't reach the pre-Covid average until May of 2023 (if we started in September 2021).


Breakdown by contract area

All of Sydney is not the same, and that is equally true with our bus network. The Sydney bus network is broken down by elements known as contract areas, which constitute different parts of greater Sydney. Using the data at hand, we can break down the first graph by contract area and see how Covid has hit each area differently.

One thing to keep in mind is that contract area 6 was changed in 2018, so for this, we refer to it as the same name throughout for consistencies sake. If you want to know more about the contract areas and which parts of Syndey each part entails, you can refer to this map here.

Firstly lets look at how to break down the data given, and peel out Adult bus trips by contract area. For the purpose of clearer data, lets split them into two different categories, inner and outer Sydney. For this, we will take areas 6, 7, 8, and 9. Then we label them inner Sydney. The rest of the areas will be labeled as outer. The main reason is that there are wildly different usage numbers between these two groups and should be compared internally.

Things we can take away from this data

This data can show us two main things: How the bus network behaves (predictably) during an outbreak and how the network recovers. One thing to take into account is that on one hand, we went to 0 cases after the outbreaks, whereas with Delta, that will probably not be the case (per our living with Covid strategy). We can see from before that if we recover at the same rate as before we probably won't hit parity with pre-Covid levels until 2023. On one hand, this means that fully vaccinated people might enjoy a more spacious bus trip to work for a while to come. On the other, the rational choice would be rationing the number of busses servicing each bus route in the middle of the city

From the comparison of inner and outer Syndey bus drops, we can see that different contract areas had differing drops pre and post lockdown. Thus we can see that the biggest drop and biggest recovery will be done in the inner Sydney section, with less variance shown in lower population areas.
